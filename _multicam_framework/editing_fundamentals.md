# Editing Fundamentals — Baseline Knowledge

**This document exists so you don't need to be taught basic editing.**
Read this before building any EDIT list. These are not suggestions — they are
the fundamentals that every editor knows on day one.

---

## Walter Murch's Rule of Six (Priority Order)

Every cut decision should be evaluated against these six criteria, in order:

1. **Emotion (51%)** — Does this cut feel right emotionally? This overrides 
   everything. If it serves the emotion, small technical problems don't matter.
2. **Story (23%)** — Does this cut advance the narrative? Every edit should 
   move the story forward or clarify something about the subject.
3. **Rhythm (10%)** — Does the cut land at a rhythmically interesting moment? 
   Not metronomic — varied, musical, alive.
4. **Eye Trace (7%)** — Where is the viewer looking? Does the cut respect 
   their focus or jolt them?
5. **2D Plane (5%)** — Screen direction, 180-degree rule, spatial orientation.
6. **3D Space (4%)** — Physical continuity of objects and people.

**Key insight**: Emotion + Story = 74% of every cut decision. If you're 
debating a cut, ask "does it feel right?" and "does it serve the story?" first.

## Interview Editing Fundamentals

### Never Cut Mid-Thought
A thought is complete when the speaker has finished their point — not when 
they pause, not when a sentence grammatically ends, but when the IDEA is done.

Signs a thought is incomplete:
- Speaker is listing things ("I like dancing, piano, guitar,")
- Speaker is building to a conclusion ("because...")
- Speaker paused but is about to continue the same idea
- The next few words complete or punctuate the point

Signs a thought is complete:
- Speaker shifts to a new topic
- There's a natural energy drop / reset
- Speaker takes a breath and changes direction
- The statement stands on its own as a complete idea

**This is NOT just about punctuation.** A period in a transcript doesn't 
always mean the thought is done. A comma doesn't always mean it isn't. 
READ the content. Understand the IDEA. Then cut.

### The Invisible Cut
The best cuts are ones the viewer doesn't notice. They should feel like 
natural transitions, not interruptions.

- Cut on motion or energy shifts, not in dead air
- Cut when the speaker's energy naturally drops between thoughts
- Don't cut in the middle of a gesture or head movement (you can't see 
  this yet, but note it for when you can)

### B-Roll as Seam Hider
B-roll isn't just decoration — it's a tool for:
- Covering jump cuts between non-sequential audio
- Bridging two takes that don't visually match
- Providing visual context for what's being discussed
- Giving the viewer's eyes a break from a talking head

### J-Cuts and L-Cuts (Split Edits)
- **J-Cut**: Audio from the NEXT clip starts before its video appears.
  Creates anticipation. "Hear it, then see it."
- **L-Cut**: Audio from the CURRENT clip continues after cutting to new video.
  Creates continuity. "See the new, hear the old."
- These make transitions feel natural instead of robotic.
- Essential for interview-to-B-roll transitions.

### Audio Continuity
- Room tone / ambient sound should be consistent across cuts
- Abrupt silence between clips is jarring — natural room tone bridges gaps
- If the audio environment changes (outdoor → indoor), the cut needs 
  to acknowledge that visually too

## Interview-Specific Rules

### Handle the Interviewer's Questions
The viewer never hears the questions. So:
- Response openers that reference the question ("Well,", "Yeah,", "So,") 
  sound orphaned → trim them (context-dependent, see intelligent_cut.md)
- If she repeats the question in her answer ("You asked about dancing — 
  I love dancing"), the repetition might be useful as context or might be 
  redundant. Decide based on whether the viewer needs the setup.

### False Starts and Restarts
People stumble. Don't throw away good content because of a stumble:
- Option 1: Splice — keep both good halves, cut the stumble
- Option 2: Trim in-point — start at the clean restart
- Option 3: Trim out-point — the first part was complete enough
(See intelligent_cut.md for full decision framework)

### Letting Moments Breathe
- After an emotional statement, DON'T immediately cut to the next clip
- Let the pause sit. Silence after "it changed my life" is powerful.
- A 1-second pause after something heavy is not dead air — it's emphasis.
- Conversely, upbeat/energetic moments should cut tighter — match the energy.

### The "Watch It Cold" Test
Before delivering an edit, imagine someone watching it who knows NOTHING 
about the project. Ask:
- Can they follow the story from start to finish?
- Do they understand who this person is within 10 seconds?
- Does every clip earn its place, or is something confusing without context?
- Would they feel something at the end?

## What Makes a Cut "Intelligent"

An intelligent cut is NOT:
- Following rigid rules without thinking
- "Always remove X" or "never include Y"
- Making the same decision every time regardless of context

An intelligent cut IS:
- Reading the transcript and UNDERSTANDING what she's saying
- Evaluating each potential segment on its own merits
- Considering how each clip relates to the ones before and after it
- Making judgment calls based on emotion, story, and rhythm
- Knowing when to break a "rule" because the content demands it

**The rules in this document and intelligent_cut.md are guardrails, not 
railroad tracks.** They tell you what to CHECK, not what to DO. The 
decision is always contextual.
